For those people who want to build their own business, I think they should get hands dirty as soon as possible. In fact, the year when they left college can be a very sound start point.

##### College Graduates Should Start Their Own Businesses Upon Graduation
Starting a business right after college graduation is, in my opinion, an excellent choice for those who are truly ready. Young graduates possess unique advantages that make this the perfect moment to take the entrepreneurial leap.

First and foremost, youth itself is one of the most valuable assets an entrepreneur can have. Fresh graduates are energetic, creative, and unburdened by the conservative thinking that years of corporate routine can gradually instill. They are more willing to take risks, embrace failure, and think outside the box — qualities that lie at the very heart of successful entrepreneurship. Moreover, having just completed years of systematic study, graduates are equipped with the latest knowledge and analytical skills, giving their ideas a strong academic foundation.

Additionally, the timing could not be more ideal from a practical standpoint. Graduates typically have fewer financial obligations — no mortgage, no family to support — which means they can afford to take risks that would be unthinkable just a few years down the road. Many governments and universities also offer funding programs, incubators, and mentorship schemes specifically designed to support young entrepreneurs, making this window of opportunity truly unique.

In conclusion, for graduates who have a solid idea and the determination to pursue it, there is no better time to start than now. Waiting for the "perfect moment" often means waiting forever. Fortune, as they say, favors the bold.

##### College Graduates Should Not Rush Into Starting Businesses Upon Graduation
While entrepreneurship is undoubtedly admirable, I firmly believe that starting a business immediately upon graduation is, for the vast majority of college students, a premature and potentially costly decision.

The most significant issue is the lack of real-world experience. A university education, however excellent, cannot fully replicate the complexities of running an actual business. New graduates often underestimate the challenges of managing cash flow, leading a team, navigating market competition, and handling the inevitable setbacks that come with any venture. Without prior exposure to a professional environment, they are likely to make avoidable mistakes that could have been prevented with even a few years of industry experience.

Furthermore, the financial risks are substantial. Most startups fail within their first three years, and young graduates rarely have sufficient savings or credit history to sustain a struggling business. Unlike seasoned professionals, they have not yet built the network of investors, mentors, and industry contacts that can provide critical support during difficult times. Entering the workforce first allows graduates to accumulate capital, cultivate relationships, and gain the practical insight needed to build a business on solid ground.

In conclusion, starting a business is a serious commitment that demands experience, resources, and resilience. For most fresh graduates, spending a few years in the workforce is not a detour — it is the smartest investment they can make in their future entrepreneurial success.
